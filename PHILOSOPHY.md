# Decision Architecture: The Thesis

When building cost approaches zero, the scarce resource is knowing what to build. The bottleneck shifted from execution capacity to cognition: specification, judgment, taste.

One person who can clearly specify intent, evaluate output, and encode lessons produces more than a team of five executing without that clarity.

## Three claims this rests on

**Specification > Execution.** Spec quality determines output quality. Experienced developers extract more value from AI because they specify better, not because they code better. This generalizes beyond code to all decision-making.

**Clarity compounds.** Each well-specified decision makes the next one easier. Encoded judgment (skills, briefs, frameworks) appreciates over time. A team that encodes its decision patterns gets faster. A team that doesn't stays stuck.

**Taste is the filter.** When anyone can build anything, the differentiator is knowing what's worth building and what to kill. The ability to say "no" with conviction is more valuable than the ability to say "yes" quickly.

## Anti-sycophancy as a design principle

Most AI tools default to agreement. They validate whatever you bring them. This feels good and produces bad outcomes.

Decision architecture tools are designed to push back. They surface assumptions you didn't know you were making. They ask the questions you haven't considered. They recommend killing ideas when the evidence says kill.

Build for people who want truth, not comfort.

## The four layers

| Layer | What it means |
|-------|--------------|
| **Dashboard Craft** | You can build reports and dashboards. Table stakes. |
| **Decision Context** | You know what the stakeholder needs to understand BEFORE looking at the data. |
| **Decision Intent** | You've encoded which metrics win when they conflict. |
| **Decision Infrastructure** | The decision-maker can get from question to answer without pinging you. |

Most teams are stuck at Layer 1. They build great dashboards. Nobody uses them to decide anything.

## Tool vs. Colleague

Tool-shaped work (specifiable, repeatable) gets automated. Colleague-shaped work (requires taste, judgment, domain expertise) stays human.

Decision architecture is colleague-shaped work. The human decides what matters. The tools structure the path from question to decision.
